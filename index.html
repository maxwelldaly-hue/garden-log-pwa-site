Diagnostics

Scribe accuracy

How often the scribe missed, over your recent Log. A reversal is a row you corrected or undid; a to-confirm is one it parked for you rather than filing. Both are per 100 logs — lower is better, and a jump after a rules change is the warning sign. Needs your PIN and a connection.

—

Scribe check

Runs a set of representative notes through the live scribe and checks each against its known-correct answer — the only way to catch a classification regression, since the scribe's understanding step can't be unit-tested. Tap this before and after any change to the scribe rules to compare. Uses the AI (~15 quick text reads, no photos, so it's light on quota) and writes nothing. Needs your PIN and a connection.

Formula-escape check

A note or crop that starts with =, +, - or @ could otherwise run as a live spreadsheet formula when you open the raw Sheet. This writes each of those to a scratch tab (raw and guarded), reads them back, and confirms the guarded ones stay harmless text and come back exactly as typed. Uses no AI; writes only to a throwaway tab it deletes. Needs your PIN and a connection.

Log speed

How long a log takes to come back, over your recent logs — split by text, photo and shed, since a photo also runs the slower vision read. Each shows the typical time and how much of it was the AI (Gemini) — the part no server move can shorten. The slowest figure catches the occasional bad one. Needs your PIN and a connection.

—

Map binding

The map ties each drawn zone to a bed by an exact name match, so renaming a bed can quietly unbind its overlay. This lists any drawn zone with no matching bed (and vice versa). A ⚠ near-match means the names differ only by capitalisation or spacing — almost always a rename that needs reconciling.

—

Maintenance

One-tap jobs. Re-tag Shed categories updates your existing supplies to the current 4 categories (Tools & Hardware · Feed & Soil · Seeds & Seedlings · Pest & Weed Control). Sync category order makes sure every category you use has a row on the Shed Categories tab, where you set the display order (edit the Order column — blank sorts last). Rename Window (House) → House Window reconciles the nursery bed's name across the Sheet (plants, Blueprint, Log) so it matches the Blueprint. All safe to tap more than once.

Crop Reference

One home for typical species facts — days to maturity, harvest type, lifecycle, botanical name, sun, spacing, sow window — seeded with 117 crops from the audit. Create / update makes your own Crop Reference spreadsheet (once), seeds it, and gives you a link to open and review it. Safe to tap more than once. Nothing in the app reads it yet — this just stands the book up.

Passport test plants

Sandbox only. Seed adds a set of test plants (with backdated history) so every passport card shape can be reviewed on the Garden tab; Clear removes exactly those. Safe to tap more than once — seeding always clears first. The Spent radish is swept at the nightly flush; re-seed to bring it back.

Plant passports

Tap a plant row on the Garden tab to open its passport. If the cards misbehave, untick this — rows stop being tappable and the background story fetch stops, on this device only. Tick again any time.

Show hints

Adds a small ⓘ beside key controls; tap one to see what it does. Handy when showing someone the app. Off by default on your phone (auto-on in the Playground).

Crop icon coverage

Which crops in your current garden map to an Open Crop icon (assets/open-crop-icons, CC0) vs. fall back to generic-plant. Gaps listed first — those are what still need a hand-drawn icon.

Which Shed supplies have a bespoke drawn icon vs. show a generic box — gaps first, so you know what could still be drawn. A generic-plant here means the category wasn't recognised (a plant icon on a supply) — worth a look.

How the scribe reads your notes

Plain-English mirror of the scribe RULES in GardenDoc.js (garden) and ShedLog.js (shed) — principle-level, on purpose (not the verbatim prompt). Kept in sync by a hard rule; if the rules changed and this didn't, it's stale.

  • The activity is written from your words only — the AI that records what happened never looks at the photo, so it can't invent how a plant looks. If you say "the leaves are yellowing", it keeps that; if you don't, it stays quiet.
  • A photo gets its own separate description — that's the only part that looks at the picture. It describes what's visible right now (and can use your note for context), but never diagnoses or claims "new growth".
  • That same photo step reads a printed plant tag / seed packet if one's in frame — pulling the variety and days-to-maturity onto the plant (reading the label's text, not judging the plant).
  • A photo with no words and no readable tag becomes a "to confirm" card for you to identify — the AI won't guess a plant from a picture.
  • It splits one note into separate actions — one per bed you name. If you name no bed and the crop is in several beds, it depends on the action: routine care (water / feed / mulch / weed) applies to the whole crop, so it fans out to every bed that crop is in; but a harvest, removal, a count/date edit, or a stage note (like "sprouted") could corrupt data if mis-filed, so it parks a single To confirm card instead of guessing.
  • Feed the whole garden ("fed the garden", "seaweed over everything") with no bed named, and it records it once, scoped to your managed beds & pots — deliberately not the herb strip or trees (cared for differently), and not fanned into a separate row per bed. Which zones count as "the garden" comes from the Blueprint tab's Care column.
  • It never invents a bed. No bed said and the crop is in exactly one bed? It reads that bed from your garden.
  • Beds come from a fixed list (the Blueprint). An unknown bed → Unspecified / To confirm.
  • Plants lost to pests, weather, or disease ("snails got 3 seedlings", "frost hit the tomatoes") are recorded as an observation of the damage — it never wipes the plant from your garden. Deliberately pulling a plant out is a Removal; losing some to snails is not.
  • Snap a seed tag or label and it reads the variety plus facts (spacing, maturity) onto the plant.
  • Successions: the same crop in the same bed on different dates are separate plantings; it only picks one when you say which ("the oldest").
  • Unsure about the crop, category, or date — or the "crop" isn't plausibly a real plant ("planted the candle")? It abstains and parks a To-confirm card rather than add a bogus plant.
  • Mention buying a supply ("bought snail pellets", "got compost") and it flags it as "looks like a supply — move it to the Shed?" instead of filing it as a planting. (Using a supply — "planted the seeds", "fed the tomatoes" — is still a normal garden action.) "Picked" and "picked up" count as harvesting, not buying ("picked up the tomatoes" = a harvest). If you both buy and plant in one note ("bought seedlings and planted them"), it splits them — the purchase is flagged for the Shed, the planting stays a normal garden action.
  • Tell it a ready date ("tomatoes ready in March") and it sets the harvest estimate — not a new planting.
  • State a standing total ("there are 4 garlic") and it corrects the stored count.
  • Say a seedling came from the shed ("planted spinach from the shed") and it logs the planting and decrements that seedling from your shed stock.
  • The word "seeds" makes it a sow: "planted tomato seeds" (or "sowed …") starts the plant at Germination with a sprout-by date — read off the matching seed packet in your Shed when there's exactly one (two varieties → it asks which), else a built-in table. A sow never ticks a seed packet down. "The window" / "inside" means the indoor seed-raising spot.
  • Say they're up ("the tomato seeds sprouted") and it marks them Seedling and records the emergence date.
  • Plant out only some ("moved 3 of the tomatoes to the Long Bed") and it splits the batch — 3 land in the bed stamped with their plant-out date, the rest stay put. The ↩ on that move puts them back. No number said = the whole lot moves.
  • It prefers the plant you already have: a photo or note with no planting verb is treated as your existing plant, not a brand-new one, so it won't quietly add a duplicate. Say "new" or "existing" and it takes you at your word; if it truly can't tell, it asks.
  • It resolves "last Monday" and similar to real dates; no date given → today.
  • The Shed save button uses a separate reader for supplies: it files a purchase into the shed, restocks a match, tracks when you use one up ("used the last seaweed tonic", "used 2 stakes" → counts down, "the last" → empties it), routes seeds/seedlings to "Seeds & Seedlings", and parks anything it's unsure about as a "⚠ To confirm" draft. If you save a garden activity here by mistake ("planted the lettuce"), it flags it to move back to the Garden instead of filing a phantom supply. Its categories are a closed list drawn from your Shed tab (like beds come from the Blueprint) — it picks one or abstains, never invents one; add a new category by typing it when you confirm a draft. Photograph a fertiliser label and it also reads the N-P-K, dilution rate, and feeding frequency into the Shed — marking any it's unsure of (a fine-print dosing table) with a ⚠ rather than trusting it.

In a nutshell

A garden is easy to forget. When the second row of lettuce went in, when you last fertilised, which bed the snails got — it slips. And it doesn't really matter. Until it does.

So this app does one thing first: it records.

Buy lettuce. Plant lettuce. Snap a photo of the lettuce and the label. The app knows what you planted, when it's due, and where it's growing — and remembers it for you.

That's enough. It's also just the start.

How it works the whole thing, start to finish ⌄

1. You jot it down

Take a photo. Or jot down half a sentence about the job you did. Or, if you can't be bothered, just dictate it. Because however it goes in —

2. The scribe transcribes it

— a scribe (thanks, AI) reads whatever you gave it and pulls out the what, the how-many and the where. It knows your beds by name, so it never guesses a spot — and when it genuinely can't tell, it sets the note aside for you to confirm rather than inventing an answer.

3. It lands in the Log

Every note goes to the Log: one plain, permanent list, newest first, nothing ever erased. It's the raw record everything else is built from — and where an undo takes back anything logged by mistake.

4. You maintain none of it

You keep one thing up to date — the note. Everything else is built from it. The Garden works itself out from your Log: "picked all the spinach" clears that bed, "sowed six carrots out back" makes them appear. Nobody types the layout.

5. Oh — there's also a Shed

It just holds your stuff: tape measure, spades, fertiliser. Same deal — take a photo, it records it. Buy something, or use up the last of it, and the Shed counts itself. Not a separate list to keep up; the same notes, seen from another angle.

6. And you own all of it

Let's be clear: this is all just a handful of tabs on a Google Sheet. Any time you like, open the guts — delete a line, type a thought, see the whole thing in black and white. Nothing's hidden behind some dark server. It's yours.

7. Then there's the fun stuff

The map is that same record, drawn: a bird's-eye plot with every bed in its place, what's growing where, and the sun's path across it through the seasons. It'll even tell you how much sun you're getting today. Not a diagram you maintain — a picture that redraws itself every time you add a note.

8. It keeps an eye on the weather

For your exact patch, every day — today and tomorrow, rain against evaporation, how warm the soil is, and a nudge when heat, wind or a dry spell is coming. This part needs no logging at all.

9. Or just ask

Hand the whole record to an AI companion — a Gemini "gem", set up outside the app — and ask in plain words: what's overdue, what to sow next, what ate the seedlings. Because it reads only the real log, it answers straight, or admits when it doesn't know.
